Tom is an applied economist. His research focuses on the following areas:

  • Methods for causal inference
  • Regional economic policy
  • Supply and demand in the health care labour market, including task substitution between types of labour (skill mix)
  • The economics of addiction
  • The effects of financial incentives in health care
  • The measurement of inequalities in chronic disease

He is interested in how policies and organisational arrangements affect the behaviours and outcomes of individuals and institutions, and how these impacts can be estimated using microeconometric methods.

Tom is or has been a co-investigator on over £13m of externally awarded research funding, from funders including the NIHR, MRC, the DHSC Policy Research Programme and NHS England. His current externally funded projects include:

The effects of regional economic policy on economic and health outcomes

Tom leads a quantitative evaluation of the economic, equity and health effects of regional economic policy (community wealth building), using modern econometric approaches including generalised synthetic control and Callaway and Sant'Anna difference-in-differences (NIHR169449, £1.6m).

The effects of care continuity under task substitution from physicians to nurse practitioners in primary care

Tom is a co-investigator on a study estimating the effects of nurse practitioner continuity of care on patient outcomes and health care use under task substitution (chronic disease management), using propensity score matching and large-scale linked individual-level primary and secondary care data with national coverage (NIHR Three Schools follow-on prevention award, £100k).

The economics of addiction

Tom co-leads the drug and alcohol theme of the Lancaster NIHR Mental Health Research Group, quantifying the economic and health consequences of co-occurring acute mental health and addiction disorders (NIHR502773, £11.3m).

Leadership and policy impact

Tom is co-Director of Research in the Division of Health Research at Lancaster University, and a member of the NIHR Public Health Research Programme Funding Committee, providing economics expertise. He is co-convenor of the economics network for the NIHR School for Public Health Research, and co-ordinates the international health economics mailing list on JISCMail. He is an Honorary Fellow of the University of Manchester and an Affiliate of The Work Foundation, a labour-market and employment think tank.

His research has informed national policy and NHS decision-making. A public health resource allocation formula he co-developed was adopted by the Department of Health as the basis for target allocations of the public health grant from 2016, and his evaluations have been commissioned by and reported to bodies including NHS England, the Department of Health and the Home Office.
